This front is a sample too, so instead of today's headlines here's the kind of analysis a politics digest trains you to hear.

First: coalitions rarely collapse over the issue named in the press release. Watch instead for the junior partner that stops sending ministers to routine committee meetings — attendance is the cheapest honest signal in politics, and it decays weeks before any formal rupture. When you read that a government fell suddenly, someone wasn't counting empty chairs.

Second: budget season is the real election. Whatever a government says it values, the appropriations line is where it votes on itself. A sample digest would walk you through one budget fight, name who traded what, and leave you with the one number that predicts the next fight.

Third: watch procedural votes, not final votes. Final votes are theater performed for constituents; procedural votes are where majorities actually flex. An opposition that wins a scheduling motion has found a crack worth watching.

That's the flavor. A real politics front pulls from the wire services and analysts you choose, in whatever country mix you follow, ranked by the editorial rules you set. What to watch: whether the empty chairs fill back up.
