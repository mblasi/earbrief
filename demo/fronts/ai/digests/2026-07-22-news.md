Good morning — this is a sample episode, so today's stories are evergreen ones, but the shape is exactly what your daily digest looks like.

First story: quantization keeps eating the stack. Every few months someone shows that a model served in four-bit precision loses almost nothing on the benchmarks people actually care about, and every few months the surprise is smaller. The technical takeaway hasn't changed: inference is memory-bandwidth-bound, so halving the bytes per weight roughly halves your cost floor. If you are still serving in sixteen-bit precision by default, you are paying a nostalgia tax.

Second: the KV cache remains the quiet villain of long context. Teams keep discovering that their latency problem is not compute but the gigabytes of attention state dragged along with every long conversation. The pattern to watch is cache reuse — prefix caching, prompt caching, whatever your provider calls it — because the first token you don't recompute is the cheapest token you will ever serve.

Third, and flag this one as a deep-dive candidate: speculative decoding has gone from paper trick to default setting. A small draft model guesses, the big model verifies, and you get two to three times the tokens per second without changing a single output. The reason it works says something deep about how redundant language is.
